MLB Hall of Fame Criteria - An Analytical Approach¶

Author: Benjamin Davidson¶

Introduction¶

In this tutorial I will provide a walkthrough of the entire Data Science pipeline all the way from data retrieval to building a machine learning classifier! The application will be to analyze criteria for players being inducted to the MLB Baseball Hall of Fame. There are 20,000 humans to step foot in the MLB but only a few hundred are deemed worthy of the Hall of Fame. What are the main features of a baseball player that makes them exceptional? In the real world, the decision for who gets inducted is left to members of the Press who each year get a ballot and subjectively choose the players they deem worthy. In this page I will take a data driven approach to define an unbiased criteria for Hall of Fame worthiness and use these results to build a classifier to predict future Hall of Famers.

Now that the data has been downloaded and is housed in a pandas dataframe, it is time to wrangle the data to fit our needs. For the purpose of formalizing Hall of Fame criteria the goal is to compose a table that houses all relevant career stats for each player throughout history. The dataset structure separates various types of stats into different tables. For instance, batting, fielding, and pitching each have their own table. We will want to combine these to represent a players career in one row, all in one table.

I have chosen to separate players into two distinct groups: Pitchers and Position Players. Historically in baseball there are exceptionally few players to ever pitch and play a non-pitcher position. Even fewer players to ever do this with any sort of consistency. It makes most sense to separate these players and treat them differently. They are also treated differently by pundits and have entirely different criteria for evaluation.

We also will want to add the column "HoF" for each player which will be a boolean value "True" or "False" that will define whether the player is a Hall of Famer or not. This will be necessary for the machine learning section where a classifier is built on labeled training data.

In the initial state shown above, each row represents one player's batting stats for one year. I wish to condense this table into each row representing one player's career statistics. To do this I will need to take every row for each playerID and sum the stats into one cumulative row for their career. This is valid because all statistics are numerical values where summing is valid.

One uniqueness to fielding data that poses a new challenge is that there is a new column "POS" that describes the fielders position. This is problematic because we cannot "sum" qualitative position values over a career in any meaningful way. I chose to handle this by assigning each player one career position, their most popular. This is likely how players are remembered and defined. I do this by checking the players most played position by Games Played.

The purpose of this following section is to select each player's most played defensive position. I do this by doing another groupby playerID but this time instead of summing all columns, I will sum all columns except for "POS". For position I define the function to aggregate with to be "first". This will select the position value first seen, which will be there most played position as setup above.

As the last step of the wrangling process we must define whether a player has been inducted into the MLB Hall of Fame for the sake of machine learning and classification. I do this by creating a dictionary that maps every MLB player in history to a boolean that will be "True" if they are a Hall of Famer and "False" if they are not.

In [10]:
# Initialize dict
is_hof = dict()

# Set all values to False initially, to be revised below
for player in df_batting_and_fielding_career['playerID']:
    is_hof[player] = 0
    
# Loop through the Hall of Fame table from our dataset to obtain info on HoF Induction status.
for row in df_hof.iterrows():
    # If the player has been inducted AND the player is in our dictionary, set their value to true.
    # The second condition of being in the dict is necessary because not all Hall of Fame inductees
    # are players that we care about for our analysis. Some inductees are managers, executives, or
    # other non-player roles in baseball.
    if row[1]['inducted'] == 'Y' and row[1]["playerID"] in is_hof.keys() and row[1]['category'] == 'Player':
        is_hof[row[1]["playerID"]] = 1
         
# Create a 'HoF' column that is set to the values of our dictionary.
# The tables will align because the dictionary was initialized in the ordering
# of 'playerID' in the career stats table.
df_batting_and_fielding_career['HoF'] = is_hof.values()

# Our resulting table with HoF values!

Exploratory Data Analysis¶

In this section we will do some initial exploratory analysis of the data we've cleaned in order to investigate suspected relationships between the data. We will use some libraries to assist with regression as well as data visualization. Visualizing the data is a great way to help us with EDA as we can spot relationships in the data much easier in well composed graphs than in very large tables.

Let's begin our investigation into what we think may cause players to be thought of as Hall of Fame quality. Players who are consistently good are much more likely to be inducted than those who are exceptional for a short period of time. For this reason I believe time in the league is a very important quality. Let's begin by exploring how games played (position players) and innings pitched (pitchers) effect hall of fame induction.

The strategy I'm choosing to use will be to calculate the percentage of players who are Hall of Famers in certain ranges of Games Played. The bins will be of width 300 so I will calculate how what percentage of players with 0-300 games played, 300-600 games played etc. have been inducted into the Hall of Fame.

In [14]:
# Array to hold the HoF percent per 'bin'
pct_of_hof = []

# Used to define the range of values for bins
max_games = df_batting_and_fielding_career['G_b'].max()

# For each bin (defining width of 300 games)
for i in range(0, max_games, 300):
    # Filter to dataframe to include only players with games played within the range for this bin
    bin = df_batting_and_fielding_career[(df_batting_and_fielding_career['G_b'] > i) 
                                       & (df_batting_and_fielding_career['G_b'] <= i + 300)]
    
    # The total number of players in this bin
    num_players = bin.shape[0]
    # Hall of famers in this bin
    num_hof = bin[bin['HoF'] == 1].shape[0]
    
    # Calculate the % of hall of famers in this bin and append to the array.
    pct_of_hof.append(num_hof / num_players)

# Plot the percentage of hall of famers against games played
plt.plot(range(0, max_games, 300), pct_of_hof)
plt.xlabel("Games Played")
plt.ylabel("Percentage of Hall of Famers")

Now that we have established that quantity matters for players, is it also true that quality matters? It is common to define the "best" players as the position players that hit the most home runs or the pitchers who allow the least runs. Below I will visualize a relationship between important statistics per game to see if the best performing players, regardless of number of games played, get inducted into the Hall of Fame.

Let's begin with observing hits per at bat by position players. This is similar to the common stat "batting average" and defines what percentage of at bats (times going up to the plate to hit) which result in the player getting a hit! To avoid abnormal errors and edge cases I will only include players who have gotten at least 50 hits in the MLB. It is safe to assume that a player with less than 50 hits is not an MLB Hall of Fame caliber position player.

Hypothesis Testing and Machine Learning¶

In this section we will use Hypothesis Testing to quantify the extent to which certain statistics relate to hall of fame induction. For contuity we will use some of the same statistics from the previous section. After, we will use Machine Learning to help us make a classifier that can predict future hall of famers based off of their statistics!

The first step to hypothesis testing is to select the right test. For this application it's correct to use a Two-Sample T test. This takes two differing samples (HoF vs. not HoF) that you would like to see if there is a difference in values (statistics) of that sample. We can quantify how confident we are that the average statistics of hall of famers exceed those by hall of famers. We can also dial in the value of how much the statistics are exceeded by!

In the code we will isolate statistics into two samples, those for hall of famers and those for non hall of famers. A Two-Sample T test will then be performed to check if the mean statistic of HoFers exceeds that of non HoFers by a specified value, 0 if we want to check if it exceeds at all (we suspect this from EDA).

# Import a method to perform a Two-Sample T test from statsmodels module
from statsmodels.stats.weightstats import ttest_ind

# extract the column of career hits by hall of famers
hits_hof = df_batting_and_fielding_career[df_batting_and_fielding_career['HoF'] == 1].H
# extract the column of career hits by non hall of famers
hits_not_hof = df_batting_and_fielding_career[df_batting_and_fielding_career['HoF'] == 0].H

# Perform Two sample T test.
# Provide the two samples
# Difference between means is "larger" than "0"
# "usevar=unequal" to say that we can't assume the variances are equal (they probably aren't)
t, p, df = ttest_ind(hits_hof, hits_not_hof, alternative="larger", usevar="unequal", value=0)
print("We can be {}% confident that the average HoF player has more hits than the average non HoF player"
      .format(100 * (1 - p)))

# Same test but check if the HoF mean is > non-HoF by 2000
t, p, df = ttest_ind(hits_hof, hits_not_hof, alternative="larger", usevar="unequal", value=2000)
print("We can be {}% confident that the average HoF player has 2000 more hits than the average non HoF player\n"
      .format(100 * (1 - p)))

Results and Discussion¶

Model Performance:¶

Wow! These numbers are very surprising, our model is incredibly poor for Hall of Fame players but it is exceptional at predicting Non-HoF players. This shows us that the model is conservative in predicting hall of famers, why may this be?

I believe part of the problem is that there are very many exceptional players that have not been entered into the HoF for a few reasons:

1)¶

Inductions are incredibly selective. Somewhere between 2-5 players are inducted each year. There are about 1,000 active players in the MLB at any given time! That means that something has to give, there will be great players that won't be inducted that will throw off our model.

2)¶

Players must wait 5 years after retirement to be eligble for induction. This means that there are future hall of famers with hall of fame quality stats that are labeled as non-hall of famers in our training set. This mislabeling will throw off our model.

3)¶

Real life hall of fame induction is a human process that takes intangible factors into account when selecting new inductees. Notable MLB writers vote for who they think deserves to be inducted each year. They will take into account (consciously or not) intangibles like leadership, attitude, effort, and communication that is not (and cannot) be quantified in our dataset. The lack of this knowledge will throw off the model and decrease accuracy.

For these reasons the model is very conservative in its Hall of Fame award. Repairing these issues are challenging. The only bullet that could be potentially fixed is #2. We could remove all data from players that have not been retired for at least 5 years. The problem with this approach is that we will be erasing decades of data from thousands of players and relying on historic data from over a decade ago. The game of baseball is ever-changing and we would be losing that if we removed data. It's tough to say which approach would yield a better result and is something that could be debated.

Academic projects almost always are designed such that they work out nicely and for that reason there is an expectation of a great result. When I realized that the model is very conservative and has flaws that is something that was difficult to deal.

Overall though, this endeavour was very enlightening and gave me hands on experience with taking a project from start to finish, which is incredibly rewarding.
